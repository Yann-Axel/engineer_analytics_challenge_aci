# Executive Recommendations
## Air Cote d'Ivoire — 12-Month Roadmap

**To:** CEO, CFO, Executive Committee
**Analysis period:** Last 12 months · **Date:** May 2026

---

## Current situation at a glance

| Metric | Result | Reading |
|:-------|:------:|:--------|
| Revenue | **$241 M** | Strong |
| Profit margin | **80.1 %** | Excellent |
| Seat load factor | **73.7 %** | Good, but room to improve |
| On-time flights | **66.7 %** | 1 in 3 flights is late — unacceptable |
| Cancellations | **3.6 %** | Concentrated on 5 high-risk routes |
| Customer satisfaction | **Negative** | Early warning signal |

> **Bottom line:** Air Cote d'Ivoire is profitable, but its operational reliability is eroding customer loyalty and leaving money on the table.

---

## What the airline should do next — and why

### Priority 1 — Fix the 5 most disrupted routes *(40 % of effort)*

**What to do:**
Launch an operational task force on the Abidjan–Lagos, Abidjan–Dakar, Abidjan–Accra, Abidjan–Ouagadougou, and Abidjan–Lomé routes, which account for the highest rates of cancellations and delays.

**Why now:**
These 5 routes show cancellation rates between 5 % and 7 % — twice the network average of 3.6 %. Passenger complaints focus on crew, baggage, and delays: internal, fixable problems. By comparison, the Lagos–Abidjan route achieves the same margin (84–85 %) with only 2.3 % cancellations. Demand is there; operations are failing to deliver.

**Expected impact:**
~30 cancellations avoided per year = **$200,000 in direct savings** + immediate improvement in brand perception.

---

### Priority 2 — Win back the 20 highest-value at-risk customers *(35 % of effort)*

**What to do:**
Personally reach out to the 20 key accounts showing signs of disengagement (declining travel frequency, unresolved complaints) and offer targeted commercial gestures.

**Why now:**
These 20 customers represent **$13.6 M in cumulative revenue** ($682,000 each on average). All have filed at least one complaint; more than half show measurable dissatisfaction. Losing half of them would cost **$700,000/year on a recurring basis**. Retaining a customer costs 5 to 7 times less than acquiring an equivalent new one.

**Expected impact:**
Keeping 10 of these 20 customers = **$700,000/year in secured recurring revenue**.

---

### Priority 3 — Upsell economy travelers to business class *(25 % of effort)*

**What to do:**
Offer personalized upgrade proposals to the 48 passengers identified as receptive to premium offers, with attractive fares tailored to their travel profile.

**Why now:**
These passengers accept upgrade offers at a rate of 17.5 %, versus 11.6 % across the network — 1.6x more likely to convert. They travel frequently and have already demonstrated a preference for comfort. This is the fastest growth lever to activate without major investment.

**Expected impact:**
**$1.5 to $3 M in additional ancillary revenue** over 12 months.

## 90-day targets

| Target | Today | Goal |
|:-------|:-----:|:----:|
| Cancellations on 5 problematic routes | 5–7 % | **< 3.5 %** |
| High-value at-risk customers | 20 | **<= 10** |
| Premium offer acceptance rate | 17.5 % | **>= 20 %** |
| Network on-time performance | 66.7 % | **>= 72 %** |

---

## Why this order of priority?

```
Stabilise first --> Retain next --> Grow last
```

Investing in growth (new routes, new customers) without fixing operational issues is like filling a leaking bucket. Flight reliability is the prerequisite for any credible commercial strategy.

---

## Risks to monitor

- **Rainy season (June–August):** weather disruptions in West Africa are partly unavoidable. The task force must target operational causes (crew positioning, aircraft rotations), not weather events.
- **Premium offers:** cap at 5 upgrade proposals per customer per year to avoid over-solicitation and NPS degradation.
- **Synthetic data:** the margins presented (78 %) are computed on simulated data. Real production margins will likely be tighter, which makes Priority 1 even more critical.
